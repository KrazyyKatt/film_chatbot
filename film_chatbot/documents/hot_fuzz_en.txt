HOT FUZZ (2007) — FILM OVERVIEW
================================================================

BASIC INFORMATION
-----------------
Director:           Edgar Wright
Screenplay:         Edgar Wright and Simon Pegg
Production:         Working Title Films / Big Talk Productions
Distribution:       Rogue Pictures (North America), Universal Pictures (International)
Premiere:           13 February 2007 (UK)
Running time:       121 minutes
Genre:              Buddy cop action comedy
Budget:             USD 12–16 million
Box office:         USD 80.7 million (worldwide)
Country:            United Kingdom
Language:           English


BRIEF DESCRIPTION
-----------------
Hot Fuzz is a British action comedy following Nicholas Angel, an exceptionally
skilled London police officer whose performance makes the rest of his team look
bad. Rather than rewarding him, his superiors transfer him to the quiet rural
village of Sandford in Gloucestershire. What seems like a dull reassignment
soon turns into a string of mysterious deaths and shocking secrets hidden behind
the idyllic village life.

The film is the second entry in Edgar Wright and Simon Pegg's "Three Flavours
Cornetto" trilogy (alongside Shaun of the Dead and The World's End), and is the
most commercially successful of the three.

PLOT — DETAILED OVERVIEW
-------------------------

1. LONDON — THE TRANSFER
   Nicholas Angel is the top-performing sergeant in the Metropolitan Police.
   He is so good at his job that he makes everyone else on the force look
   inadequate. Instead of being rewarded, his superiors collectively decide
   to transfer him to the sleepy village of Sandford in Gloucestershire,
   far from any real action.

2. ARRIVAL IN SANDFORD
   Angel arrives in Sandford — a tidy, peaceful village where almost nothing
   happens. Upon arrival he arrests Danny Butterman for drunk driving, only
   to discover that Danny is the son of local Inspector Frank Butterman and
   a police officer himself. Angel is frustrated by the village's mundanity:
   incompetent colleagues, trivial "crimes," and the Neighbourhood Watch
   Alliance (NWA) whose obsession with low crime statistics takes precedence
   over actual law enforcement.

3. THE FIRST MURDERS
   Angel and Danny pull over two lead actors from a local production of Romeo
   and Juliet for speeding. Shortly after, a cloaked figure murders both actors
   with an axe and stages their deaths as a car crash. Angel is the only one
   who suspects foul play. Danny begins to follow Angel's lead and take crime
   more seriously.

4. A SERIES OF KILLINGS
   The murders keep coming. Angel and Danny confiscate an illegal weapons
   stash — including an old sea mine — from a local farmer. Wealthy land
   developer George Merchant is killed in a gas explosion at his home. At the
   village fête, journalist Tim Messenger is killed by masonry dislodged from
   the church tower, just as he was about to share information with Angel.
   Florist Leslie Tiller is murdered with her own garden shears while Angel's
   back is turned.

   Angel suspects supermarket manager Simon Skinner — a planned property deal
   would have built a rival supermarket, giving him motive — but Skinner has
   an alibi.

5. UNCOVERING THE CONSPIRACY
   After concluding there must be multiple killers, Angel is attacked in his
   hotel room by Skinner's employee "Lurch." He overpowers him and learns of
   a secret NWA meeting at Sandford Castle. There, Angel is confronted with a
   shocking truth:

   The NWA — led by Inspector Frank Butterman — is behind all of the murders.
   Each victim had in some way threatened Sandford's chances of winning the
   annual "Village of the Year" competition. Frank's motive is deeply personal:
   his late wife Irene had devoted herself to winning the inaugural competition,
   but travellers moved in the night before the judges arrived and ruined their
   chances, driving Irene to suicide. Frank subsequently vowed to ensure
   Sandford won the award every year, no matter the cost.

   Angel flees but stumbles into the castle's catacombs, where he discovers the
   bodies of the NWA's victims. Danny suddenly appears and appears to "kill"
   Angel — but is in fact saving his life by faking it, and whispers to Angel
   to escape to London for his own safety.

6. THE RETURN AND THE SHOWDOWN
   Angel heads for London, but at a motorway service station he spots a display
   of Danny's action movie DVDs — and turns back. Arming himself with the
   confiscated weapons from the police station, Angel launches into an epic
   shootout with the NWA across Sandford. Danny joins his partner in the fight.
   Together they convince the other officers of Frank's guilt.

   Frank flees. He and Skinner escape in a police car, leading Angel and Danny
   on a high-speed chase and shootout. Angel corners Skinner at the village's
   miniature model park, where after a fight Skinner is impaled on the steeple
   of a miniature church — but survives. Frank, attempting to escape in Angel's
   car, is attacked by the swan the two had recaptured earlier, causing him to
   crash.

7. EPILOGUE
   Professor Tom Weaver, the last remaining NWA member, appears in the squad
   room wielding a blunderbuss. He fires at Angel, but Danny takes the hit.
   In the ensuing struggle, Weaver accidentally activates the confiscated sea
   mine, killing himself and destroying the entire police station.

   One year later: Angel has been promoted to Inspector, and Danny — having
   survived — has been promoted to Sergeant. After visiting Irene's grave
   together, the two drive off towards their next crime scene as true partners.


THEMES AND STYLE
----------------
Hot Fuzz is simultaneously a parody and a love letter to action movies. Pegg
emphasised that the film is not a mockery of the genre but rather looks up to
it with genuine affection. The film skewers genre clichés — blazing shootouts,
high-speed chases, dramatic conspiracy reveals — but does so with warmth and
sincerity rather than sneering superiority.

A central comic tension is the contrast between the dull, picture-postcard
English countryside and the explosive mayhem of Hollywood action films. The
film contains direct references to Point Break, Bad Boys II and Lethal Weapon,
as well as The Wicker Man (1973) — a British horror film about a dark secret
hidden within an isolated community, which is a clear parallel to Sandford.

The gore and visual effects are deliberately over-the-top and stylised,
reinforcing the comedic tone: the more extreme the violence, the more absurd
and funny it becomes — similar in spirit to the Black Knight scene in Monty
Python and the Holy Grail.


PRODUCTION
----------
- Wright and Pegg spent 18 months writing the screenplay. They watched over
  138 cop-related films and conducted more than 50 interviews with real police
  officers for research.
- Principal photography lasted 11 weeks in Wells, Somerset (Wright's hometown),
  which stood in for the fictional Sandford. Wells Cathedral was digitally
  painted out of every shot, as Wright wanted the Church of St Cuthbert to
  serve as the town's central landmark.
- While on location in their uniforms, Pegg and Frost were regularly stopped
  by passers-by who mistook them for real police officers and asked for
  directions.
- The mansion explosion was achieved using gas mortars to create large fireballs,
  while the final destruction of the police station was depicted by blowing up
  a miniature model.
- After editing, Wright cut approximately 30 minutes of footage from the film.


CRITICAL RECEPTION AND AWARDS
-------------------------------
- Rotten Tomatoes: 91% approval rating (average score 7.7/10)
- Metacritic: 81/100 — "universal acclaim"
- Empire Awards 2008: Won — Best Comedy
- Empire magazine (2016): Ranked 50th on the list of 100 Greatest British Films
- Empire magazine (2020): Ranked 67th on the list of the 100 Greatest Films
  of the 21st Century
- New York Times Readers' Choice (2025): Ranked 108th on the "100 Best Movies
  of the 21st Century" list


BOX OFFICE
----------
- Opening weekend in the UK (14 February 2007): £7.1 million
- Opening weekend in the US (20 April 2007): $5.8 million from just 825 cinemas
  — the highest per-cinema average of any film in the top ten that week
- Total worldwide gross: $80,573,774
  (nearly double what Shaun of the Dead earned in the US, and more than three
  times its gross in other countries)


SOUNDTRACK
----------
The score was composed by British composer David Arnold, known for scoring
the James Bond film series from 1997 to 2008. The broader soundtrack blends
British rock from the 1960s and 70s (The Kinks, T. Rex, Sweet, Dire Straits),
new wave (Adam Ant, XTC), and Glasgow indie band The Fratellis. The track
"Here Come the Fuzz" was specially composed for the film by Jon Spencer's
Blues Explosion.


TRIVIA
------
- Hot Fuzz is the second film in the "Three Flavours Cornetto" trilogy
  (Shaun of the Dead → Hot Fuzz → The World's End). Each film features a
  Cornetto ice cream as a recurring motif.
- Edgar Wright appears in an uncredited cameo as a shelf-stacker at the
  Somerfield supermarket — a job he actually held in real life.
- Peter Jackson (director of The Lord of the Rings) appears as a deranged man
  dressed as Father Christmas.
- Cate Blanchett has an uncredited cameo as Angel's ex-girlfriend Janine,
  appearing only in protective goggles and a forensics mask.
- Nick Frost agreed to appear in the film only on the condition that he could
  name his own character — he chose "Danny Butterman."
- Edward Woodward's role as Professor Weaver is a deliberate nod to The Wicker
  Man (1973), in which he played a law-abiding police officer — a clear parallel
  to Angel's character.
- The title was inspired by the two-word naming style of 1980s and 90s action
  films (e.g. Lethal Weapon, Point Break, Executive Decision). Wright has said
  he wanted a title that "really had very little meaning."
