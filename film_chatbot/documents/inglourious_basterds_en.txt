INGLOURIOUS BASTERDS (2009) — FILM OVERVIEW
================================================================

BASIC INFORMATION
-----------------
Director:           Quentin Tarantino
Screenplay:         Quentin Tarantino
Production:         The Weinstein Company; A Band Apart; Zehnte Babelsberg Film GmbH
Distribution:       The Weinstein Company (US); Universal Pictures (International)
Premiere:           May 20, 2009 (Cannes Film Festival)
Wide release:       August 21, 2009 (United States); August 20, 2009 (Germany)
Running time:       153 minutes
Genre:              Black comedy war film / alternate history
Budget:             USD 70 million
Box office:         USD 321.5 million (worldwide)
Countries:          United States / Germany
Languages:          English, German, French


BRIEF DESCRIPTION
-----------------
Inglourious Basterds is a 2009 alternate-history war film written and directed
by Quentin Tarantino. Set in Nazi-occupied France during World War II, the film
follows two separate plots to assassinate the Nazi leadership that converge at
a Paris cinema. One plot is driven by a team of Jewish-American soldiers known
as "the Basterds," led by Lt. Aldo Raine; the other by Shosanna Dreyfus, a
young French-Jewish woman who survived the massacre of her family and now runs
a cinema in Paris under a false identity.

Both storylines are shadowed by the film's central antagonist, SS Colonel Hans
Landa — one of cinema's most celebrated villains — played by Christoph Waltz
in an Oscar-winning performance.

The film deliberately rewrites history, ending with the assassination of Hitler
and the Nazi high command — an act of cinematic wish-fulfillment that generated
both acclaim and controversy.

PLOT — DETAILED OVERVIEW
-------------------------
The film is structured in five chapters.

--- CHAPTER ONE: ONCE UPON A TIME IN NAZI-OCCUPIED FRANCE ---

In 1941, SS Colonel Hans Landa — known as "The Jew Hunter" — arrives at the
rural French farm of Perrier LaPadite. In a masterfully tense, extended scene
conducted partly in French and partly in English, Landa interrogates LaPadite
about a fugitive Jewish family, the Dreyfuses, who are hiding under the
farmhouse floorboards. He offers LaPadite amnesty for his own family in
exchange for information.

LaPadite breaks down and confirms where the Dreyfuses are hiding. Landa's men
open fire through the floorboards, killing the entire family. One girl —
young Shosanna Dreyfus — escapes through a window into the open countryside.
Landa watches her flee but deliberately lets her go, calling after her with
a chilling "Au revoir, Shosanna."

--- CHAPTER TWO: INGLOURIOUS BASTERDS ---

Three years later, in 1944, U.S. Army First Lieutenant Aldo Raine assembles
a team of eight Jewish-American soldiers for a covert operation behind enemy
lines in occupied France. He calls them "the Basterds" and makes his mission
clear: they will kill Nazis, and each soldier owes him 100 Nazi scalps.

The group includes:
- Donny "The Bear Jew" Donowitz, who beats Germans to death with a baseball bat
- Hugo Stiglitz, a former German sergeant who murdered 13 Gestapo officers
- Corporal Wicki, an Austrian-born Jewish translator
- Several other Jewish-American soldiers

The Basterds quickly develop a fearsome reputation among German forces, partly
through the scalping of killed soldiers and partly through leaving one survivor
alive — branded with a swastika carved into their forehead — to spread terror.

--- CHAPTER THREE: GERMAN NIGHT IN PARIS ---

Shosanna, now living under the false identity of "Emmanuelle Mimieux," operates
a cinema in Paris. She catches the eye of Fredrick Zoller, a celebrated German
war hero whose single-handed killing of 300+ Allied soldiers has been turned
into a Nazi propaganda film, "Nation's Pride," directed by Joseph Goebbels.

Zoller is infatuated with Shosanna and convinces Goebbels to hold the film's
premiere at her cinema. Shosanna, recognizing this as an opportunity for
revenge, devises a plan with her Afro-French lover and projectionist Marcel:
they will lock the Nazi leadership inside the auditorium and burn it down using
her stock of highly flammable nitrate film, with Shosanna's pre-recorded face
projected on screen to announce her identity to those about to die.

Meanwhile, British intelligence recruits Lt. Archie Hicox — a film critic and
fluent German speaker — for "Operation Kino": infiltrate the premiere with the
Basterds and kill the Nazi leadership. They are to meet their contact, German
film star and secret Allied spy Bridget von Hammersmark, in a tavern.

--- CHAPTER FOUR: OPERATION KINO ---

Hicox, Stiglitz, and Wicki meet von Hammersmark in a cellar tavern in the
occupied town of Nadine. Unfortunately, a group of German soldiers is
celebrating there, and an SS officer, Dieter Hellstrom, becomes suspicious
of Hicox's unusual German accent. A card game begins, tensions mount, and
Hicox unknowingly uses a non-German hand gesture when ordering three drinks
— flashing three fingers instead of using the thumb and first two fingers.
His cover is blown.

A Mexican standoff erupts into a massacre. Everyone is killed except a
wounded von Hammersmark and one German soldier, Wilhelm. Aldo Raine
negotiates with Wilhelm for von Hammersmark's release — which she ends by
shooting Wilhelm herself.

Raine is suspicious of von Hammersmark but she convinces him she is loyal
and delivers crucial information: Hitler himself will be attending the premiere.
Raine, Donowitz, and Ulmer will go in disguise as Italian filmmakers.

Meanwhile, Landa investigates the tavern massacre, finds von Hammersmark's
lost shoe and a napkin with her signature — evidence she was involved.

--- CHAPTER FIVE: REVENGE OF THE GIANT FACE ---

The premiere begins. The cinema is filled with Hitler, Goebbels, and the
entire Nazi high command, surrounded by German soldiers.

Multiple events unfold simultaneously:

SHOSANNA'S PLAN: Marcel prepares to ignite the pile of nitrate film behind
the cinema screen. Shosanna sets up her pre-recorded message to play before
the fire begins. However, Zoller follows her to the projection booth,
confronting her about her coldness toward him. Shosanna, unable to suppress
her hatred, shoots him. In a moment of ambivalence, she moves to check on him
— and he shoots her in return. Both die in the booth.

THE BASTERDS' MISSION: Donowitz and Ulmer hide explosives in the opera box,
strapped to their legs. Raine and Utivich, however, are arrested by Landa,
who has seen through their Italian cover. Landa, who has been quietly
surveilling the entire conspiracy, now reveals his true calculation: he has
no loyalty to Hitler or the Reich. He contacts Raine's OSS commanders and
negotiates a remarkable deal — he will allow the attack to proceed and
surrender himself to the Allies in exchange for guaranteed immunity, a
pardon, a medal, and a comfortable retirement on Nantucket Island.

THE CLIMAX: As the Nation's Pride screening reaches its bloody climax,
Shosanna's pre-recorded face suddenly appears on screen, announcing to
the horrified Nazi audience that they are about to be killed "by a Jew."
Marcel lights the nitrate film. The cinema explodes into an inferno.

Donowitz and Ulmer burst into the opera box and empty their machine guns
into Hitler and Goebbels, then fire wildly into the panicking crowd.
The timed explosives detonate, killing Donowitz, Ulmer, and everyone
remaining in the cinema. The entire Nazi leadership is wiped out.

THE ENDING: Landa drives Raine and Utivich to Allied territory, where
they surrender to Raine as agreed. The OSS confirms Landa's deal by radio.
Raine — honoring the spirit but not the letter of the agreement — shoots
Landa's radio operator dead. Then, in the film's final scene, he carves a
swastika into Landa's forehead with his knife.

Landa, horrified, asks how he'll ever hide this.

"I got a feeling the war's over for you," Raine replies. He steps back,
studies his work, and delivers the film's final line:
"You know... this just might be my masterpiece."


CHARACTER NOTES
---------------

HANS LANDA (Christoph Waltz)
The film's most memorable character. A polyglot Austrian SS colonel who speaks
French, English, German, and Italian with equal fluency, Landa is charming,
theatrical, highly intelligent, and utterly without moral scruple. He does not
hate Jews out of ideology — he simply chose to hunt them because it made him
exceptional at his job. Tarantino initially believed the role was "unplayable"
and feared no actor could pull it off; when Waltz auditioned, Tarantino said
he "gave me my movie." The character is widely considered one of cinema's
greatest villains.

SHOSANNA DREYFUS (Mélanie Laurent)
The film's emotional core. Shosanna is driven purely by grief and a desire
for revenge. Unlike the Basterds — who kill for ideology and sport — her
motivation is personal. She carries the film's moral weight, and her plan is
ultimately what destroys the Nazi leadership. Tarantino described her as a
"Jewish Joan of Arc." Notably, many of her character attributes were originally
developed for "The Bride" in Kill Bill, which Tarantino worked on first.

ALDO RAINE (Brad Pitt)
A coal miner and bootlegger from the Great Smoky Mountains of Tennessee who
previously fought the Ku Klux Klan and survived a lynching. Raine is loud,
brash, and darkly comic — his demand for 100 Nazi scalps and his terrible
attempts at foreign accents are among the film's most entertaining elements.
He is based loosely on the archetype of the determined, righteous American
warrior in classic WWII films.


THEMES
------
The film is an exercise in alternate history as cathartic fantasy: what if
the war ended not with years of further bloodshed but in a single night,
in a burning cinema, through the acts of the powerless and overlooked?

Tarantino structures the film as a spaghetti Western transposed to WWII —
complete with chapters, extreme close-ups, tense standoffs, and a
Morricone-influenced score. Cinema itself is central: the film ends inside
a cinema, burned down by the very medium it celebrates. Film is portrayed
as both a tool of Nazi propaganda (Nation's Pride) and the instrument of
Nazi destruction (Shosanna's nitrate fire).

The film raises uncomfortable questions about revenge, violence, and
moral equivalence. Some critics praised the cathartic power of depicting
Jewish characters as agents of violent revenge; others criticized it for
reducing the Holocaust to genre entertainment.


PRODUCTION
----------
- Tarantino began writing the script in 1998 but struggled with the ending
  for years. He considered it his potential masterpiece and "became too
  precious about the page." He diverted to make Kill Bill (2003-2004) and
  Death Proof (2007) before finally completing Basterds.

- The script reportedly had three nearly complete drafts by 2002 with no
  working ending. Luc Besson eventually convinced Tarantino to finish it
  as a film rather than a miniseries.

- Leonardo DiCaprio was originally sought for the role of Hans Landa before
  Tarantino decided to cast a native German-speaking actor. Christoph Waltz,
  then largely unknown to American audiences, got the role.

- Adam Sandler was cast as Donny Donowitz but had to drop out due to scheduling
  conflicts with Funny People. Eli Roth took the role.

- Simon Pegg was Tarantino's first choice for Lt. Archie Hicox but had to
  withdraw due to The Adventures of Tintin. Michael Fassbender (who originally
  auditioned for Landa) was cast instead.

- Nastassja Kinski was approached for Bridget von Hammersmark; when negotiations
  failed, Diane Kruger was cast.

- Rod Taylor came out of retirement specifically for the role of Winston
  Churchill at Tarantino's personal invitation. This was his final film
  appearance; he died in January 2015.

- Principal photography began in October 2008 in Germany, primarily at
  Babelsberg Studio in Potsdam and in Bad Schandau, near the Czech border.

- The theater fire scene nearly became a real disaster: the fire burned at
  1,200°C (2,190°F) instead of the projected 400°C (752°F), almost
  incinerating the cast and crew. The swastika on the screen also collapsed
  unexpectedly when its steel cables softened in the heat.

- For the strangling scene, Tarantino reportedly strangled Diane Kruger
  himself (with her consent) in one take to ensure authenticity.

- Ennio Morricone was originally intended to score the film but was
  unavailable due to prior commitments. Tarantino instead used eight of
  Morricone's existing compositions, along with music from other sources
  including David Bowie's "Cat People (Putting Out Fire)."

- The title's deliberate misspelling ("Basterds" instead of "Bastards,"
  "Inglourious" instead of "Inglorious") was a creative choice by Tarantino,
  who described it as "a Basquiat-esque touch." He has never given a fuller
  explanation, saying simply "that's just the way you say it."

- The final script was leaked online before release, with fan sites posting
  excerpts and early reviews.

- The German version of the film is 50 seconds longer than the American
  version, with an extended tavern scene included in the main cut rather
  than as a bonus feature.


CRITICAL RECEPTION
------------------
- Rotten Tomatoes: 89% positive (average 7.8/10)
  Consensus: "A classic Tarantino genre-blending thrill ride, Inglourious
  Basterds is violent, unrestrained, and thoroughly entertaining."
- Metacritic: 69/100 — "generally favorable reviews"
- CinemaScore audience grade: A−

At Cannes, the film received an 8–11 minute standing ovation after its first
screening. Critics were divided: many praised it as Tarantino's best work
since Pulp Fiction; Roger Ebert called it "big, bold, audacious"; others found
it morally troubling or self-indulgent.

The most significant critical debate centred on the film's portrayal of Jewish
violence and its revisionist treatment of the Holocaust. Some Jewish critics
objected to the spectacle of Jewish soldiers mirroring Nazi brutality;
others questioned whether the film trivialized the Holocaust by turning it
into genre entertainment. Tarantino rejected these readings, describing the
film as "not a Holocaust movie."

SOUNDTRACK
----------
Tarantino originally wanted Ennio Morricone to compose an original score, but
scheduling conflicts prevented this. Instead the film uses eight of Morricone's
existing compositions (four on the CD release), alongside other eclectic choices:
the opening theme is "The Green Leaves of Summer" from the 1960 film The Alamo;
David Bowie's "Cat People (Putting Out Fire)" plays over a key Shosanna sequence;
and the overall sound blends spaghetti Western, R&B, and other genres in classic
Tarantino fashion. The soundtrack was the first of Tarantino's not to include
dialogue excerpts.

TRIVIA
------
- Tarantino has called the opening scene — Landa interrogating LaPadite —
  his "favorite thing" he has "ever written."

- Leonardo DiCaprio was originally sought for the role of Hans Landa. The
  role later made Christoph Waltz an international star.

- Adam Sandler was originally cast as Donny Donowitz. He dropped out due to
  scheduling conflicts with Funny People (2009).

- Simon Pegg was first choice for Lt. Hicox but dropped out; Michael
  Fassbender, who had originally auditioned for Landa, took the role instead.

- Donny Donowitz is, within Tarantino's shared universe, the father of Lee
  Donowitz — the obnoxious film producer played by Saul Rubinek in
  Tarantino's True Romance (1993).

- Rod Taylor, who played Winston Churchill, was fully retired and had no
  agent when Tarantino personally tracked him down and persuaded him to
  return. The film was his last before his death in 2015.

- The theater fire scene in the film's climax nearly became a genuine
  disaster on set when the fire burned at three times the predicted temperature.

- The German version is 50 seconds longer than the US cut.

- Tarantino originally considered making the film as a TV miniseries due to
  its length. Director Luc Besson convinced him to trim it to a feature film.
